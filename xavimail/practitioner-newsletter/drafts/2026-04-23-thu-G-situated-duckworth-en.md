Angela Duckworth is on LinkedIn this week promoting her next book. It's called *Situated*. The argument: situation shapes who you are and what you're capable of — more than the personal development world has acknowledged.

She's not wrong.

This is the thread I've been pulling at for a while now — why situation matters, what it does to a person, what it activates or suppresses. Tuesday's email on the Scientific Reports paper is part of that conversation. So is a lot of what I've been writing about lately.

What's interesting is who's saying it. Duckworth wrote *Grit*. That entire book was a theory of the person: develop more passion, more perseverance, push through. Now she's saying the situation is sometimes the variable. That's a significant shift from someone who built their reputation on the opposite.

*Situated* will do something important: it will put this argument in front of millions of people who've never considered situation a factor at all. That matters. More people will show up to our work ready to think this way.

What it won't do: tell you which situations fit which person. She'll argue that situations matter. She won't have a way to map a specific person's nature against a specific arrangement and say — this costs you, here's why, here's what would cost less.

That's what we're already doing.

I've been working this angle more directly in coaching over the past several months. Not just "is your nature aligned with this work" — but "is this situation structured in a way your nature can actually run in." Different question. People who'd completed the MNTEST, understood their nature, were still stuck. This got underneath it. The results have been different.

When *Situated* lands in September, clients will read it, or hear about it. This is useful context for that conversation: yes, situation matters. And there's a way to get specific about which situation, for which person.

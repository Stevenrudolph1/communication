Hi {first_name},

You've seen this with your own clients.

Same person, two different roles. In one they're sharp, present, hard to stop. In the other they're doing everything right — working hard, staying professional — and still ending every week depleted.

Most frameworks explain this by looking at the person: their attitude, their resilience, their relationship with pressure. But the person hasn't changed. What's changed is the demand.

Every situation has a structure. It asks for specific kinds of energy in specific amounts — sustained focus or rapid response, coordination with others or protected solo time, visible output or quiet maintenance work that nobody sees. That structure exists before the person arrives. It doesn't adjust to who shows up.

When someone's natural supply meets the situation's demand, the work has a different quality. Not easier — but cleaner. Recovery happens faster. The depletion curve is shallower.

When it doesn't match, everything still functions. But it costs more. And the cost compounds quietly until something gives.

As a practitioner, this is your first move: look at what the situation is asking for before you decide what's wrong with the person.

Most of what your clients think is personal is structural.

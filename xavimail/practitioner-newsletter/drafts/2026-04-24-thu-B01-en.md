Every Thursday I'm looking at one career through the MN lens — what it actually demands, who tends to thrive in it, and what mismatch looks like from the inside.

This week: **wilderness guide.**

**What the role demands**

A wilderness guide leads people through unfamiliar terrain, often under physical and environmental pressure. The work requires reading landscape and weather, managing group safety, making fast decisions with incomplete information, and staying composed when conditions change without warning. There's no predictable schedule, no office, and no controlled environment. The situation changes. You adapt or you fail.

**Who tends to thrive**

The **Adventurous Nature** is the obvious fit — people energized, not worn down, by physical uncertainty and environmental variability. But the MI picture is more specific: **Gross Bodily Intelligence** (moving through terrain fluently, managing the body under load), **Naturalistic Intelligence** (reading the environment — weather, risk, animal behavior, plant markers), and **Spatial Visual Intelligence** (holding the landscape in mind, planning routes dynamically without a map in front of you).

**What mismatch looks like**

A guide who has strong Gross Bodily MI but a more Administrative Nature may be technically excellent and still find the sustained unpredictability exhausting after a few seasons. They'll handle the physical demands without complaint. It's the uncontrolled environment — the constant adaptation — that accumulates.

The mismatch rarely shows in the first year. It shows in year three, in the preference for the familiar loop, the reluctance to take the variable route, the sense that the job used to feel different.

Not a motivation problem. A structural one.

---

If you have a client considering this kind of work, the question isn't "can you handle the outdoors?" It's "what does sustained physical uncertainty cost you — and what does it give you?"

Structure vs. Alignment

She was the best person on the team. Everyone said so. Clients asked for her by name. When something broke, she fixed it. When a decision needed to be made and nobody was sure, she made it.

She was also exhausted.

So the obvious question: is she in the wrong role? Is the work not right for her? Maybe she needs something that fits her better.

That's a reasonable question. But it might be the wrong one to ask first.

There are two very different things that make a competent person feel depleted. They look almost identical from the outside. But they need completely different responses, and if you mix them up, the intervention fails.

The first is structural. The decisions route through her because no one ever established who decides what. The knowledge lives in her head because it was never written down anywhere else. The coordination happens through her because no system was built to hold it. She's not doing her job. She's doing her job plus the work that three missing systems should be doing.

The problem isn't her. It's what was never built around her.

The second is alignment. The work itself — the actual type of engagement it demands — doesn't match how she naturally operates. She can do it. She does it well. But it asks her to be administrative when she's creative, or relational when she's analytical, or steady when she's exploratory. The structure around her might be perfectly fine. The fit isn't.

Both of these show up as the same thing: someone who's good at their work and tired of it.

Here's where it matters for practitioners.

When someone is carrying structural load — when they're compensating for systems that don't exist — it changes how they engage. They start operating in modes that aren't natural to them, because the situation demands it. They look more administrative than they are. More protective. More controlling. Not because that's who they are, but because that's what the absence requires.

If you assess their natures in that state, you get a distorted picture. You're not reading the person. You're reading the compensation.

And then every recommendation that follows is slightly off. Not wrong enough to be obviously wrong. Just wrong enough to not work.

So the sequence matters. Check for structure first. Always.

If you redistribute the load and the depletion lifts, it was structural. The person was never misaligned. They were just carrying what shouldn't have been theirs to carry.

If you redistribute the load and the depletion stays, now you know. Now the alignment question is real. And now you can see their actual engagement pattern clearly enough to work with it.

Getting it backwards is one of the most expensive mistakes a practitioner can make. You end up exploring someone's nature fit while the real problem is that three jobs collapsed into one person and nobody noticed. Or you help someone reorganize beautifully, and they're still tired, because better systems don't fix wrong-nature work.

The distinction sounds simple. Structure is about load. Alignment is about fit. Check structure first.

But in practice, this is the thing that separates a practitioner who names the right problem from one who names a plausible one. Your clients feel the difference.

I'd be curious whether this connects to something you're already seeing in your own work.
